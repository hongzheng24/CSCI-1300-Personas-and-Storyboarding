Designing effective and accessibile UI/UX involves a process of observation, interviewing, personas, and storyboards. This project follows such process, focusing a Walmart's Kiosk Self-Checkout and its interface.

Preparation

First, we need to prepare. We need to select an accessible interface, generate objective questions and create a rough sketch of the interface.
  1. Interface: Walmart Self-Checkout Kiosk
    2. A self-checkout kiosk is a safe interface to observe. It's public, meaning there's no weird stares when observing people, and many different types of people use it everyday. It also had multiple components to it that are important for a smooth checkout process.
  2. Questions
    • Do you prefer this method (self-checkout) or regular checkout..why?
    • What goes through your mind when you see the layout of the screen?
    • Was there any point were you felt calm/rushed by the process or none at all?
    • If the computer acted more like a human, would you say the process would have the same, less, or better effectiveness?
  3. Sketch
    4. This interface, which aims to assist users in checking themselves our for their groceries, has a main component and two sub grids (labeled). The main section displays itsm and their prices. The first sub section displays all scanned items and the total price and the last sub displays assistive buttons that include volume and language controls.

Observation

The observation process of this is pretty simple: we just need to obvserve people (passive) using the interface and interview them (active) about their experience using the interface.
Passive Observation
  • Some users focused more on the product than the interface
  • Users often waited for the interface to respond (screen change or noise) in order to continue.
  • Users who ran into problems where an employee had to help were older
  • Those who got the frustrated the most were younger
  • A few people use the assistive buttons
  • Most people were slowed down by the payment screen
Active Observation
  • Self-checkout is often preffered because the process is faster regardless of how long the line is.
  • Based on experience with other checkout processes, Walmart's interface can bring feelings of familiarity, complacency, pressure, or nothing.
  • Most users feel calm (sometimes even meditative or boring) in the process
  • Most users said "humanizing" the computer would "defeat the purpose" while others mentioned they like to feel like they're interacting with a human.
  • Users view the process as "medium risk medium reward": if you know what you're doing, it could be more efficient but if you make a mistake, it can be frustrating

Personas

Constructing personas includes a four-quadrant overview of what the person would "Feel", "Say", "Think", and "Do". Personas are built from the interviews observations gathered in the previous section and aren't based on one particular subject. Personas are FICTIONAL characters and should never represent real people.
Bob (left) the Bored man doesn't necessarily hate or love grocery shopping. He just has better things to do like watching the game. This persona is based off on the nonchalant older demographic that is featured in my observations. Bob is ok with the technology but he is easily frustrated.
Florence (right) is a young woman who is quickly trying to meal prep before going out with friends. She is very familiary with technology but is easily flustered by large crowds. This persona is based off the younger more tech-savvy demographic I observed: despite a time constraint, this demographic is more inclined to look at the details and is more empathetic toward others.
